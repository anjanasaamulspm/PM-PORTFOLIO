Section Context

What Matiks actually is

Matiks is a competitive math gaming app — founded September 2024, Bengaluru, by Sudhanshu Bhatia, Mohan Kumar, and Sushant Timmapur. The core bet: math as an e-sport, not a textbook. You drop into a 1v1 live duel within seconds of opening the app, solve arithmetic or logic puzzles under a timer against a real opponent, and climb a global ELO leaderboard.

It raised $3.14M pre-Series A in August 2025 led by Tanglin Ventures with Info Edge Ventures and a notable angel lineup: boAt's Aman Gupta, Unacademy co-founders Gaurav Munjal and Roman Saini, Scapia's Anil Goteti. The roster signals conviction that this is a real category — not a niche edtech experiment.

Live 1v1 Math Duels
ELO-matched head-to-head — DMAS, speed arithmetic, timed battles. Core loop.

Social Feed + Streaks

Creators Programme

The timing is also significant. India's Online Gaming Act (Aug 2025) banned real money gaming — Dream11, MPL, Games24x7 all took a major hit. Casual skill gaming stepped into a vacuum. Matiks is well-positioned to capture habitual gaming attention from users who can no longer bet on cricket.

    Three things that are actively eroding the experience

    The core loop works. The problems are all trust and fairness issues — and in a competitive product, trust is the product. If the leaderboard is fake, the ELO means nothing. If exits aren't penalised, winning a duel means nothing. These aren't cosmetic bugs; they threaten retention of exactly the high-skill users Matiks most needs to keep.

    🔍 Personal observation — leaderboard integrity

    I saw a public contest score of 80 questions answered in 1 minute 47 seconds. That's ~1.3 seconds per question, sustained across 80 answers. For arithmetic problems, this is humanly impossible at any reasonable difficulty — it's either calculator use, a second device, or a multi-account setup. This score appeared on the public leaderboard with no flag, no review, no asterisk.

    🔍 Personal observation — mid-game exits

    Players frequently quit mid-duel when losing. The penalty for exiting is not sufficient to disincentivise it — and the person who stays loses their time and gets a corrupted result. In a game built on competitive fairness, a rage-quit that has no real cost is a design failure.

    🔍 Personal observation — XP vs. skill

    XP accumulates through volume, not quality. A player grinding easy DMAS rounds looks identical in XP to someone winning CrossMath duels at high difficulty. This means XP is not a meaningful signal of skill — it's a measure of time spent. When low-skill players carry inflated XP, matchmaking degrades: I've been paired against opponents who couldn't answer basic arithmetic that any competitive-level player would treat as a warm-up.

    The developer acknowledged matchmaking and stability as "known issues" in a January 2026 App Store response. Known isn't fixed. And these three problems compound each other: inflated leaderboards make skilled users feel the game isn't worth winning; bad matchmaking makes every session feel random; exits without consequences remove any stakes from a match.

    Recommendation 01 Priority: High

    Fix leaderboard integrity and exit penalties

    Problem
    Impossible scores are visible with no flag. Mid-game exits have no real cost.
    Both are trust problems. A player who sees an 80-question-in-1:47 score on a public leaderboard doesn't think "that person is amazing" — they think "this game is rigged." Once that thought lands, the competitive motivation that makes Matiks work collapses. The same logic applies to exits: if quitting a duel you're losing costs nothing, every close match becomes a lottery on whether your opponent feels like finishing.
    What I'd ship first — statistical anomaly flagging: Set a per-question speed threshold for each game mode (e.g. DMAS at the 99th percentile of all verified human scores). Any contest submission that exceeds this threshold gets auto-flagged and held from the live leaderboard until a lightweight review triggers. This is a simple statistical filter on the output side, not real-time surveillance.

    Exit penalty redesign: A player who exits mid-duel should receive: (1) an immediate ELO loss equal to a full loss, (2) a 60–90 second cooldown before next match, and (3) a "forfeit" marker on their recent match history visible to them. The cooldown alone is enough to make rage-quitting feel costly without being punitive to genuine disconnects.
    North star metric: % of top-50 leaderboard scores that pass anomaly threshold · target: 95%+ within 60 days of shipping
    Left: flagged impossible score held from live leaderboard · Right: proposed exit penalty flow with ELO deduction + cooldown timer
    Why this is the highest priority The users most damaged by fake leaderboards are the highest-skill users — the ones who would become Matiks' most vocal advocates if the competitive integrity were real. Matiks cannot build a mind-sport brand on a foundation that serious players don't trust. Fix this before spending on marketing or new features.
    Recommendation 02 Priority: Medium

    Fix the onboarding drop-off for non-DMAS game modes

    The speed-to-first-duel is a genuine strength for DMAS (basic arithmetic). But Matiks has several other modes — CrossMath, KenKen, Flash Anzan, Mind Snap — that users bounce off because the app gives a few bullet points and drops them into a live match before they understand what they're even trying to do.

    ✗

    Current flow for complex modes

    3–4 bullet-point explanation → immediately paired with an opponent → confusing mid-match experience → player loses, feels stupid, exits the mode permanently.

    →

    What I'd change: mode-specific "practice round"

    Before the first live duel in any non-DMAS mode, offer one 60-second offline practice — same interface, same timer, but against dummy problems with no ELO at stake. One screen, no tutorial wall, just a live taste that ends with "Ready to play for real?"

    →

    Home screen — "Start Here" path for new users

    The home screen currently shows everything at once. For new users (first 3 sessions), pin one featured mode per day — the one with the most active players — so the first decision is made for them, not left open.

    Left: current flow drops new users straight into live duels · Right: proposed practice gate before first live match in any new mode
    The risk of leaving this unfixed Matiks' funded roadmap mentions regional language launches and classroom tools. Both rely on reaching users who are less mathematically confident than the early-adopter power users. Those users will bounce even faster at a blank "How to Play" screen. Fix onboarding for complex modes now, before scaling to a wider audience where the drop-off will be steeper.
    Metric
    Mode adoption rate beyond DMAS
    Currently DMAS dominates session starts heavily. The target: percentage of users who play at least one non-DMAS mode in their first 7 days should rise meaningfully after the practice round ships — that's the leading indicator that the onboarding fix is working before it shows up in retention.
    Watch for: % users completing ≥1 non-DMAS duel in first 7 days · baseline to be established, target +15pp within 30 days
    Recommendation 03 Priority: Medium-High

    Build a monetisation path that doesn't alienate the core

    Matiks currently has 100K users and one paid product: the Creators Programme. That's the right instinct — monetise the power users — but it's a thin wedge. The segment most likely to pay for Creators is exactly the same segment most likely to leave if competitive integrity stays broken. You can't sell a premium tournament product on a platform where the leaderboard is visibly inflated.

    What could work: Skill Pass
    Monthly subscription unlocking: detailed accuracy breakdowns, opponent performance history post-match, and practice mode for any format. Targets serious players. Priced at ₹99–149/month — well within India's gaming subscription comfort zone.
    What could work: Brand Contests
    Sponsored weekly contests with a brand prize pool (vouchers, not cash — avoids real money gaming regulations). Brands get visibility in a highly engaged, 15–30 age cohort. Matiks gets contest infrastructure that runs itself.
    What won't work: Pay-to-win anything
    Any model that lets users buy ELO boosts, skip matchmaking queues, or purchase extra challenges affecting competitive standing. Instantly destroys trust in an app already fighting integrity perception problems. The XP Booster product that exists today is a yellow flag — it must never touch ELO.
    Left: proposed Skill Pass — insight-only, no competitive advantage · Right: sequenced revenue model — trust first, then monetise

    The real monetisation unlock is scale, not product complexity. At 100K users, ad revenue from rewarded video would already cover operating costs. At 1M users, a Skill Pass at ₹99/month with even 3% conversion is serious recurring revenue. The monetisation strategy right now should be: fix retention first, grow the base, then layer subscriptions. Not the reverse.

    The sequence matters Integrity fix → Onboarding fix → Scale to 500K users → Launch Skill Pass. In that order. Launching paid features on a platform where high-skill users don't trust the leaderboard is selling premium seats on a bus with no route.

    Reflections

    📍 Why I have a 40-day streak

    I captained my university volleyball team and competed in math competitively — both are activities where the score is real, the opponent is real, and the gap between your good days and bad days is visible. Matiks is the first app that's made math feel like that again. The core concept genuinely works for me as a user. Which makes the integrity problems more frustrating, not less — I know what this could be if the competitive trust were real.

    The biggest thing I'd flag to Matiks' PM team: your most engaged users are your harshest critics — not because they want to leave, but because they care enough to notice when the game is unfair. The 40-day streak user who sees an 80-question-in-1:47 score is not going to quietly accept it. They're going to feel like their investment in the product was wasted.

    I'd also want Matiks to think carefully about what "Duolingo of math" actually means — because Duolingo's core design insight wasn't gamification, it was forgiveness. Losing a streak on Duolingo doesn't feel catastrophic because the app is warm and encouraging. Matiks is the opposite register — competitive, fast, high-stakes by design. That's the right call for a certain user, but it means the app has very little room to absorb trust damage before that user walks.

    Finally: the XP-as-volume problem is fixable without rebuilding the system. Add a secondary visible metric — accuracy-weighted ELO, or "Skill Rating" distinct from XP — that compounds quality of play, not just quantity. Show both. Let users self-select which one they care about. That single change makes XP honest without taking anything away from the users who value grinding.
