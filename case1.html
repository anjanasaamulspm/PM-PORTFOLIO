CASE 01 Live

Live QR-code offers at checkout: a discovery problem in app-based ordering

Why don't users see restaurant offers until they've already decided what to order- and what would change if they did?

JTBD RICE PR/FAQ
The problem

What I noticed as a user

Most food-delivery and e-commerce checkouts in India follow the same sequence: browse, add to cart, then- right before paying- open a new tab to search "[app name] coupon code today." That second step is a habit now, not an edge case. 62% of online shoppers actively search for a coupon code before completing a purchase[1], and for Gen Z specifically that number climbs to 78%[2]. The app already has the user's intent at checkout — and then sends them looking for a deal somewhere else.

That detour is expensive. Once a shopper leaves the tab to hunt for a code, a meaningful share simply doesn't come back: 49% of consumers report abandoning a cart when they expect a discount and can't find one[3], and average coupon searches only succeed 10–30% of the time[4]- meaning most of that detour ends in disappointment, not a saved cart.

    • Who it's for

    Jobs to be done

    Three distinct jobs show up in this moment, and they don't all want the same thing:

    Job 1: The habitual bargain hunter "When I'm about to pay for food I've already chosen, I want to know I'm not leaving money on the table, so I can check out without the nagging feeling I overpaid."
    Job 2: The price-sensitive first-timer "When I'm trying a new app for the first time, I want a low-risk reason to complete the order, so I can justify trying something unfamiliar."
    Job 3: The impulse upsizer "When I'm already checking out, I want to know if spending a little more unlocks a better deal, so I can decide whether it's worth it before I pay."

    What's notable: none of these three jobs are actually being done well by a static "Apply Coupon" text field. A blank input box assumes the user already knows a code exists — it doesn't surface anything, it just waits. The QR-code concept is one candidate answer to all three jobs at once: something visible and specific at the exact moment the job activates, instead of a passive field that only rewards people who already left to go searching.

    Why it matters

    Sizing the opportunity

    This isn't a marginal UX nitpick- it sits on top of two large, well-documented patterns. First, the scale of coupon-seeking itself: this is now mainstream checkout behavior, not a fringe habit confined to deal-hunters. Second, the cost of failing to meet that behavior: a meaningful share of abandoned carts trace directly back to a missing or unfound discount, on top of an already-high baseline cart abandonment rate.

    The food-delivery category specifically tends to sit at the lower end of cart-abandonment benchmarks compared to categories like luxury or home goods[5] — meaning the baseline intent at checkout is already high.     • Prioritization

    What I'd build first, and why

    I scored three candidate responses to this problem using RICE. The QR-code-at-checkout concept isn't the only fix- a smarter "Apply Coupon" autofill or a pre-checkout deals banner are both lower-effort alternatives — but it scored highest because it's the only one that addresses discovery (the user doesn't know a deal exists) rather than just friction (the user knows a code exists but it's annoying to enter).

    Why not just ship the highest RICE score The banner and autofill ideas score well because they're cheap, not because they solve the deeper problem. They reduce friction for users who already know to look for a deal- they don't change the 62% who currently leave the app to search. I'd treat them as quick wins to ship in parallel, and the QR concept as the bet that actually closes the discovery gap, sequenced as an MVP test on one high-traffic order flow before wider rollout.

    MVP scope: a single static QR per order that reveals one offer, tested on dinner-hour orders in one city, measuring completion-rate lift against a held-out control group- before investing in dynamic, order-aware offer logic.

    The proposal

    What I'd ship

    A QR code rendered directly on the checkout screen, just above the pay button, that reveals one relevant offer- a percentage off, free delivery, or cashback- when scanned or tapped. The offer is tied to what's already in the cart, so it reads as a reward for this specific order rather than a generic banner.

    1

    Contextual trigger

    At checkout, the system looks at cart composition and order value to decide which offer is worth surfacing- a ₹450 order sees a different nudge than a ₹120 one.

    2

    Offer reveal

    A QR code (or a single tap, if already on mobile) appears next to the total- visible, not buried in a menu- and reveals the specific offer instantly.

    3

    One-tap claim

    Claiming applies the discount to the active session immediately- no code to copy, no new tab, no leaving the order.

    Proposed checkout screen- QR offer reveal above the pay button
    PR/FAQ- if this shipped "Starting today, your checkout screen shows a deal made for this order- scan it, tap it, done. No searching, no separate tab, no expired codes. Just open the app, order, and see what you've unlocked before you pay."

    💡 Key insight: the user isn't actually loyal to coupon-hunting- they're loyal to not feeling like they overpaid. If the app removes the need to leave and search, it doesn't lose the bargain-hunting behavior, it just relocates it to a moment the app controls.

    Success metrics

    How I'd know it's working

    North star: checkout completion rate among sessions where the QR offer was shown vs. a held-out control. Everything else is a supporting or guardrail metric underneath that.

    Checkout completion rate, QR-shown vs. control
    North star
    Off-app exits during checkout (tab switches / app backgrounding)
    Leading indicator
    Average discount cost per completed order
    Counter-metric / guardrail

    The counter-metric matters most here: it's easy to lift completion rate by simply giving away large discounts. The real test of this concept is whether it lifts completion without increasing average discount depth beyond what the team would already budget for promotions- i.e., is it making existing offers more effective, not just making offers bigger.

    30 / 90 / 365-day view At 30 days: does the QR-shown cohort complete checkout at a meaningfully higher rate than control, holding order value constant? At 90 days: does that lift hold once the novelty of "a QR code at checkout" wears off, or does it decay toward baseline? At 365 days: has it changed user behavior upstream- fewer users opening a second tab at all, because they've learned the deal will show up in-app?
    Reflections

    What I'd do differently

    The biggest assumption I'm making is that a QR code is the right interaction at all- for a user already on their phone, a tap is strictly less friction than a scan, and I've kept QR mostly because it ports cleanly to a desktop checkout flow too. I'd want to validate with real users whether the "scan something on the same screen you're looking at" interaction actually feels delightful or just feels redundant on mobile.

    I'd also want real data on how much of the 62% coupon-searching behavior is price-driven versus habit-driven- if it's mostly habit, a visible in-app offer might convert it easily; if it's mostly genuine price comparison against competitor apps, no amount of in-app convenience fixes that, and the real lever is the offer's actual value, not its discoverability.

    The biggest risk is discount cannibalization- surfacing an offer to users who would have checked out at full price anyway. I'd mitigate this by only showing the QR reveal to sessions that show early signals of detour risk (e.g., have previously abandoned a cart, or are new users), rather than to every checkout by default.
